Interests

Susceptibility to infectious disease is governed by a complex set of interactions which are driven by host and pathogen genetic variation.

In my research, I am interested in developing useful statistical and experimental tools to better understand the genetic architecture of the host response to viral infection.

Through this work, I hope: to characterize novel factors that lead to severe disease (including higher-order allelic effects and parent-of-origin effects); to increase our understanding of emerging and neglected infectious diseases; and ultimately to improve global health.

About Me

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Bioinformatics and Computational Biology at UNC-Chapel Hill, working on modeling heritable host determinants of influenza A virus pathogenesis in the labs of Mark Heise and Will Valdar. I was a 2015-2016 predoctoral trainee in the molecular biology of viral diseases. I am based in the department of genetics, and since 2015, I have served on the North American board of directors for the non-profit organization Universities Allied for Essential Medicines (UAEM).

I received my masters degree in Molecular Microbiology and Immunology from the Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health. While at Hopkins, I studied flavivirus transmission in Culex mosquitoes through field work completed in Macha, Zambia, and I trained in vaccine science and policy. I obtained my bachelors degree in Biochemistry and Religion from Swarthmore College.
