Interest

Susceptibility to infectious diseases is governed by a complex set of interactions which are modulated by host and pathogen genetic variation. In my research, I am interested in developing useful statistical and experimental tools to better understand the genetic architecture of the host response to viral infection in order to characterize novel factors that lead to severe disease.

About Me

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Bioinformatics and Computational Biology at UNC-Chapel Hill, working on modeling heritable host genetic determinants of influenza virus pathogenesis. I am a predoctoral trainee in the molecular biology of viral diseases, and I am on the board of directors for the non-profit organization Universities Allied for Essential Medicines, North America.

I received my masters degree in Molecular Microbiology and Immunology from the Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health. While at Hopkins, I studied flavivirus transmission in Culex mosquitoes through field work completed in Macha, Zambia, and I trained in vaccine science and policy. I obtained my bachelors degree in Biochemistry and Religion from Swarthmore College.
